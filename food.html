Plastic Kingdom

It is cheaper to buy loose fruits and veg than plastic packed

Supermarkets sign pact to recycle but keep plastic and sell pricier items

Biggest supermarkets in the UK sign public pact to tackle plastic pollution but NGOs say it’s not enough.

by Estefania Contreras Tellechea


Some supermarkets in the UK like Tesco Metro, Lidl, Sainsbury’s and many more don’t offer plastic free options to sell products like fresh vegetables and fruit. Most of these perishable items come wrapped in plastic, inside a plastic bag or in a plastic try and some of them even come in a plastic tray inside a plastic bag.

When you enter a food shop in the UK you can see everything is pretty much plastic and when consumers shop in the fruits and vegetables section it can be easy for them to choose what already comes inside a package and has a price tag on. Some of these items are marked as premium or organic and the vast majority of them carry the supermarket’s brand.

Some products packaged with plastic are more expensive than loose vegetables and fruits, but the way they are branded and priced can make it look like they are cheaper.

For example, a customer can look at the options of broccoli in a Marks & Spencer and see one broccoli wrapped in plastic with a 1 pound sticker on it and at first, it looks like the plastic-free option, the loose broccoli, has a price of 1.35 pounds. That person could assume that the plastic one is 35 pence cheaper and put it in the trolley, but the loose broccoli is actually 1.35 per kilogram. You can actually buy double the amount of product, 2 broccoli, for the same price if you buy it lose.


Brocoli sold at a Marks & Spencer supermarket in Cardiff, Wales. Photograph: Estefania Contreras


What a person chooses to buy when they go to a supermarket is part of the throwaway culture that exists in the UK and all over the world. People are used to buying items like vegetables, fish, yogurt, bread or any other normal grocery product inside a plastic package, it seems more convenient and easier to grab.

Picking loose items instead of plastic would reduce dramatically the amount of waste generated in a household and maybe even push foodshop chains to decrease their production of plastic and also create plastic free aisles.

Besides the benefits that reducing plastic pollution would to the environment, purchasing plastic free could be more budget friendly. Depending on the item and the shop, it might be cheaper to get your fruits and vegetables loose. However, not every company has loose or plastic free options for every fresh item they offer, most of them leave the customer with the only choice of buying with plastic or going to another shop.

Even though this big food shop chains like Marks & Spencer, Tesco, Lidl, Sainsbury’s and others, signed up to be part of The UK Plastic Pact, they still offer an enormous amount of their products wrapped or packed in plastic every single day and it does not seem like they have reduced the use of plastic.

Supermarkets in the UK produce 800,000 tonnes of plastic packaging in a year. Even if it reused or recycled most of this waste ends up incinrated on landfill as litter or even in rivers and oceans, according to Greenpeace UK.

Back in April The UK Plastic Pact was announced. It is a movement that has the biggest supermarkets in the country and other big companies like Coca-Cola and Danone pledging to reduce and redesign plastic packaging.

“No one business, council, recycle or government department can create a New Plastic Economy alone. Only a common approach to polymer use, recovery and reuse will deliver the change we need. At M&S, we will play our part and are committed to reducing our plastic use and making all our packaging widely recyclable. “ stated Mike Barry, Director of Plan A at Marks & Spencer.

This agreement, organized by the charity WRAP, is looking to create a circular economy for plastic; however, Non-governmental organizations and experts say that is not enough to tackle the problem.

The pact has a goal of making 100% of all plastic packaging reusable, recyclable or compostable by 2025 but Alice Hunter, a Digital campaigner at Greenpeace UK, stated bioplastics can be just as bad as normal plastic, they can end up in the ocean and endanger sea life and recycling is just not sufficient at this point.

“Recycling is absolutely vital for dealing with waste, and we definitely shouldn’t start chucking everything in the bin for landfill. But unfortunately, our recycling systems just can’t cope with the amount of plastic we currently produce – especially as plastic production is on the increase.”, claimed Hunter.

Sainsbury’s, Tesco, Co-op, Aldi, Waitrose, Lidl, Asda, Morrison and Marks & Spencer produce tonnes of plastic packaging every year and they are not doing enough to prevent plastic waste ending in the ocean.

Some claim that plastic is useful to avoid the product from bruising or to even reduce food waste but according to Greenpeace UK, a study has shown that it could make it worse.

“When plastic packaging requires food to be a specific size or shape, edible food is rejected because it does not look the right way”, said the NGO.

Some of these companies announced that are making changes and that are committed to reducing specific plastic products but according to Hunter, supermarkets make small tweaks that are just enough to make them look good, but not enough to significantly reduce their plastic pollution.

“Supermarkets need to acknowledge the need to reduce the amount of plastic packaging on their shelves, and urgently commit to plastic reduction targets.”, said the Greenpeace campaigner.

WRAP has stated they will not publicly address or shame the organizations that do not comply with this pact, which leaves this voluntary initiative with no enforcement mechanism. This pact also fails to commit to removing all single-use packaging, instead promising to remove “problematic or unnecessary” single-use plastic by 2025.

A report by the World Wild Fund for Nature shows that 67% of plastic waste in the UK actually comes from packaging and 70% of this packaging market comes from the grocery sector.

The real solution to reduce the 10 million tonnes of packaging used in the UK every year, more than using bio-plastics, compostable or recyclable products, is that food shops stop using plastic altogether.

“Sometimes packaging is necessary – pre-chopped fruit and veg are vital for people who have accessibility issues. Stopping excess plastic at the source will mean that there’s less of it in circulation and ultimately less plastic in our oceans.

There is no evidence that these companies are reducing or completely banning plastic packaging in comparison to other companies like Iceland and Ekoplaza, which have introduced plastic-free aisles.

Co-founder of the organization A Plastic Planet, Sian Sutherland, has created this initiative of plastic free aisles and empowers the consumer to shop consciously and go plastic free.

“Recent history has proven that recycling this kind of plastic is not the answer; it is valueless, too difficult to reclaim and often contaminated. It’s fundamentally the wrong use of plastic in the first place. But right now the public has no choice but to buy their food and drink packaged in this indestructible plastic.” claims the organization on their website.

Globally, the largest market for plastic is packaging. Most of this waste never gets recycled or incinerated and represents half of all the plastic waste in the world, according to National Geographic Magazine.
